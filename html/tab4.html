Kepler Laws

Kepler's laws or laws of planetary motion are scientific laws that describe the movement of the planets around the Sun.
They are named after their creator, the German astronomer Johannes Kepler.

Previously it was believed that the planets revolved around the earth until Copernicus with his heliocentric theory showed that they revolved around the sun.
The kepler theory allowed the improvement of this theory, the kepler theories are synthetic, that is, their function is to describe the planetary movement, whose characteristics are deduced thanks to mathematical calculations.




- First law: Law of orbits -

This says that the planets revolve around the Sun describing an ellipse-shaped orbit.
The Sun is located in one of the foci of the ellipse.


An ellipse can be described as a flattened circle with two spotlights or fixed points.
The degree of flattening of a curve that is closed is called eccentricity, When the eccentricity is equal to 0, the curve forms a perfect circle, on the other hand, when the eccentricity is greater than 0, the sides of the curve are flattened forming an ellipse.

Example:



The formula:
$$ e=\frac{c}{a} $$
Where,
- it's eccentricity
- c is distance from the focus to the center
- a is the semi-major axle

The eccentricity of the Earth's orbit is 0.0167, this means that the ellipse that describes the Earth is almost circular.




- Second law: Law of the areas -

In this it is established that the vector radius sweeps equivalent areas in the same time interval. (The vector radius is an imaginary line that connects a planet with the Sun, so its length varies depending on the distance between the two)

There are two points on the path of a planet where celestial bodies reach their distances and limit speeds.

1. The perihelion is the closest point of a planet to the Sun.
2. Aphelion is the farthest point between a planet and the Sun.





- Third law: Law of periods -

This allows you to compare the characteristics of the movement of the planets with each other.
(The comparison takes into account the orbital period and the radius of orbit of each planet. - The orbital period is the time it takes for a planet to completely turn the Sun)

The formula:
$$ \frac{{T}^{2}}{{a}^{3}}= K $$
Where,
- T2 is the time or orbital period squared
- a3 is the radius or semi-major Axis of the orbit of the cube
- K is the constant

Taking into account the orbital period (T) and the radius of orbit (a) to obtain the Kepler constant (K).
